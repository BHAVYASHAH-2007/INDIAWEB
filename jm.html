Jama Masjid, also known as Masjid-i Jahān-Numā, is a magnificent Mughal-era mosque located in Delhi, India. Completed in 1656 by Mughal Emperor Shah Jahan, it stands as a testament to Indo-Islamic architecture and serves as a significant religious and historical landmark. The mosque is renowned for its impressive scale, intricate details, and its capacity to hold thousands of worshipers.

The mosque's construction began in 1644 and took twelve years to complete, employing around 5,000 workers. It is built from red sandstone and marble, with a vast courtyard capable of accommodating up to 25,000 worshippers. The mosque features three grand gateways, a main prayer hall, and four minarets, each adorned with intricate carvings and calligraphy.

Jama Masjid holds great religious significance, particularly as the principal mosque of Old Delhi and the place where Muslims traditionally gather for Friday prayers. It also serves as a popular tourist attraction, drawing visitors from around the world to admire its architectural grandeur and historical importance.
