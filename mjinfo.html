Lord Mahavira's Life and Teachings:


Born in 599 BCE in present-day Bihar, India, Lord Mahavira, originally named Vardhamana, was a prince who renounced worldly life at the age of 30 to pursue spiritual enlightenment. After 12 years of rigorous asceticism, he attained Keval Gyan, complete knowledge, and became known as Mahavira, meaning "great hero".


His core teachings revolved around the "Five Great Vows":

  • Ahimsa (Non-violence):

    This is the cornerstone of Jainism, advocating for harmlessness towards all living beings, both physically and mentally.

  • Satya (Truthfulness):

    Speaking the truth and avoiding falsehoods in all circumstances.

  • Asteya (Non-stealing):

    Not taking anything that is not freely given.

  • Brahmacharya (Chastity/Celibacy):

    Controlling sensual desires and practicing self-restraint.

  • Aparigraha (Non-attachment/Non-possessiveness):

    Minimizing attachment to material possessions and worldly things.




Celebrations:

Mahavir Jayanti is observed with great enthusiasm by the Jain community. Temples are beautifully decorated with flowers and flags. Devotees visit temples early in the morning to offer prayers and perform Abhishek (ritual bathing) of the idols of Lord Mahavira. Processions (Rath Yatra) are carried out in many cities, with the idol of Mahavira placed on a chariot. Charitable activities like feeding the poor and donating to those in need are also integral to the celebrations.


Significance:

Mahavir Jayanti is not just a religious festival; it's a reminder of the importance of living a life of compassion, morality, and non-violence. It encourages individuals to reflect on their actions and strive to embody the virtues preached by Lord Mahavira. His teachings on non-violence and simple living continue to inspire people across the world to pursue peace, harmony, and a more ethical way of life.
